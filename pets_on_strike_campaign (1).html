01 — Campaign Strategy

Feed the
Revolution

Core Concept

The campaign positions kibble not as a food category but as a symbol of societal complacency — something pet parents unknowingly accepted without questioning it. Pets On Strike reframes freeze-dried raw food as an act of informed rebellion, not a premium upsell. The purchase becomes an identity statement.

Emotional Positioning

  • Make pet owners feel like advocates, not just consumers
  • Position kibble-feeders as the silent majority who haven't woken up yet — no shame, just an invitation
  • The brand is a secret society your pet let you into
  • Every bag purchased is a vote cast for a better world for animals

Psychological Triggers

  • Identity signaling — "I'm the kind of person who reads the label"
  • Moral licensing — buying this absolves guilt about not cooking for your pet
  • In-group belonging — you're part of the revolution now
  • Disgust activation — against by-products, fillers, and vague "meal" ingredients
  • Social proof anxiety — everyone else's pets are already eating this
  • Humor as disarmament — laughter drops defenses and opens wallets
Why It Works

The Cult Brand Formula

Cult brands don't sell products. They sell worldviews. Glossier sells self-acceptance. Supreme sells scarcity. Pets On Strike sells moral clarity disguised as sarcasm. The brand gives hyper-online Millennials and Gen Z a cause that feels low-stakes (it's pet food) but emotionally high-value (it's for someone they love unconditionally).

Viral Potential

Why People Will Share

The content makes people look funny and informed at the same time. Sharing a Pets On Strike video signals: "I care about my pet," "I have taste," and "I get the joke" — three distinct social wins in one share. That trifecta is how content escapes its audience and enters the general feed.

Market Disruption

The Blank Space

Premium pet food brands are either clinical (science-y, vet-approved, sterile) or twee (hand-painted illustrations, pastel fonts, soft vibes). Nobody owns the intersection of luxury and rebellion. That white space is entirely ours.

02 — Brand Voice

How We
Sound

Trait 01

Deadpan Serious

We never wink at our own jokes. The funnier it is, the straighter we play it. The brand speaks as if the pet rebellion is completely real and entirely justified.

Trait 02

Quietly Savage

We don't yell. We don't rant. We make one surgical observation and walk away. The most devastating line in any post should feel like it just happened to you.

Trait 03

Factually Armed

Every sarcastic claim is backed by a real truth. We weaponize actual ingredient facts, processing statistics, and nutritional data — but make them feel like punchlines.

Trait 04

Internet-Native

We speak fluent meme without ever trying to sound young. We reference internet formats structurally — not by name-dropping them. The brand would never say "it's giving."

07 — Visual Direction

The Look
System

Photography
High-contrast black and white. Film grain overlay mandatory — not digital noise, film grain. Natural light only. No studio flash. Pets photographed at eye level or below, never from above. The pet is always the main subject. Shallow depth of field. Product appears in frame as incidentally as possible.
Lighting
Raking side-light for textures. Overcast window light for portraits. No ring lights. No artificial softboxes. Shadows are not enemies — use them. The goal is photojournalism, not product photography.
Typography
Bebas Neue for protest headlines — always large, always aggressive in scale. Barlow Condensed Italic for wit and secondary statements. Space Mono for data, ingredients, and legal-style text. Never mix more than two typefaces in one piece. Kerning is tight. Leading is tighter. Type should feel like it means business.
Color System
Riot Black (#0D0D0D), Pamphlet White (#F5F2EB), Concrete (#9E9B94), Asphalt (#3A3A38). No brand colors. No accent colors. Ever. The only contrast is tonal. Treat the absence of color as the most premium choice available.
Graffiti Elements
Used sparingly. Never decorative. Graffiti appears as a protest mark — a stamp, a scrawl, a stenciled slogan over an existing surface. Think Banksy placement logic: the graffiti is always ideologically placed, not aesthetically placed. A "CANCELED" stamp over a kibble bag image. A "REAL FOOD" stencil on a wall behind a dog.
Post Layout
Two formats only: (1) Full-bleed photo with minimal type overlay, bottom-anchored. (2) Pure graphic — black background, white Bebas type, one bold statement. No templates. No Canva energy. Every post looks like it was made by someone who owns a protest press.
Product in Frame
The bag appears in context, never on a white background. On a concrete floor. On a kitchen counter that looks lived-in. Next to a dog's paw. Never floating. Never hero-shot. The product has enough confidence that it doesn't need special lighting.
Luxury Rebellion
The aesthetic reference points: Raf Simons lookbooks, Supreme drops, Celine editorial, Virgil Abloh's type-on-type. The luxury comes from restraint and precision. The rebellion comes from what you say, not how chaotic the design is. Clean is radical when everyone else is cluttered.
"The loudest thing a brand can do is use silence, negative space, and one true sentence."
— Pets On Strike Design Manifesto, Section 3

System

10 — Product Integration

In the
Wild

TikTok

Incidental Hero

The bag is in frame but not the point. The pet is the point. The POS bag sits on the counter while the dog does something real. The logo is readable. That's enough. Never hold the product to camera.

Reels

Close Detail

Extreme close-up of freeze-dried pieces. Texture. Color. Then the pet's nose entering frame. The product sells itself under this kind of honest scrutiny. Let the camera stay on it too long.

Photography

Context Over Staging

The bag lives where it actually lives — near the food bowl, on a kitchen shelf, on a floor next to a dog bed. Real kitchens. Real mess. Real textures. The product doesn't need a clean background to look premium.

Stories

The Daily Check-In

Stories are where the product shows up most casually. "Today's breakfast" — one frame, the bowl with POS pieces, the dog waiting. That's it. Consistency of this format builds more brand recognition than any polished campaign.

Influencer

Process Not Product

Show rehydrating, show portioning, show the smell reaction. The process of using freeze-dried is more compelling than the product shot. The product is the prop. The ritual is the sell.

Memes

Logo as Shorthand

The P.O.S. monogram or the crossed-out wordmark becomes a meme element itself. Community starts using it as a stamp on their own content. Brand becomes a visual language, not just a product.

11 — Viral Psychology

Why They
Share

  • Identity confirmation: Sharing POS content signals "I'm a good pet parent, and a smart consumer." Two identities, one share.
  • Disgust activation: Once someone learns what 4D meat or corn syrup glaze is, they can't un-know it. The urge to share disgust is evolutionary.
  • Moral recruitment: Sharing feels like inviting others to make the right choice. It's informing, not promoting.
  • Humor as social currency: Funny pet content about a serious topic makes the sharer look socially aware and witty. That's a powerful reward.
  • Rage bait (targeted): The ingredient list content makes people angry — not at POS, but at the status quo. They share to recruit others into the outrage.
  • Nostalgia disruption: "I didn't know" is a powerful emotional state. Content that reveals something creates a before/after in the viewer's own narrative.
Curiosity Triggers

The Incomplete Statement

"We read the ingredient list on 10 kibble brands. Slide 7 made us put the bag down." The viewer has to complete the loop. They scroll. They share because they need others to feel what they felt.

Rage Bait

Surgical Fact Drops

We never rage. We inform — flatly, precisely, without commentary. That restraint makes the fact hit harder. "The FDA allows this in pet food" followed by silence is more enraging than an angry rant.

Relatability

The Hypocrite Recognition

"I read every label for myself but not for them" is a universal guilt most pet owners have felt and suppressed. When content names that guilt with humor, the relief of recognition makes people share immediately.

14 — Competitor Differentiation

The
Landscape

Brand Type Their Positioning Their Aesthetic Their Weakness
Mainstream Kibble
(Purina, Royal Canin)		 Science-backed, vet-recommended, trusted institution. Safety as selling point. Clinical, sterile, authoritative. Blue and white. Serif fonts. Institutional trust signals. Ingredients that don't survive scrutiny. Marketing depends on low consumer awareness. Ultra-processed reality hidden behind "research" framing.

(Orijen, Acana)		 Biologically appropriate, sourced ingredients, serious pet food for serious owners. Dark, natural, earthy. Photography-forward. Serious and informational. No humor.
